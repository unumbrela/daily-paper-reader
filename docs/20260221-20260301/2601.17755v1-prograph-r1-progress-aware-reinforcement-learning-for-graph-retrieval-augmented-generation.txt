Abstract 

Graph Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG) has been successfully applied in various knowledge-intensive question answering tasks by organizing external knowledge into structured graphs of entities and relations. It enables large language models (LLMs) to perform complex reasoning beyond text-chunk retrieval. Recent works have employed reinforcement learning (RL) to train agentic GraphRAG frameworks that perform iterative interactions between LLMs and knowledge graphs. However, existing RL-based frameworks such as Graph-R1 suffer from two key limitations: (1) they primarily depend on semantic similarity for retrieval, often overlooking the underlying graph structure, and (2) they rely on sparse, outcome-level rewards, failing to capture the quality of intermediate retrieval steps and their dependencies. To address these limitations, we propose ProGraph-R1, a progress-aware agen-tic framework for graph-based retrieval and multi-step reasoning. ProGraph-R1 introduces a structure-aware hypergraph retrieval mecha-nism that jointly considers semantic relevance and graph connectivity, encouraging coherent traversal along multi-hop reasoning paths. We also design a progress-based step-wise policy optimization, which provides dense learning signals by modulating advantages according to intermediate reasoning progress within a graph, rather than relying solely on final outcomes. Experiments on multi-hop question answering benchmarks demonstrate that ProGraph-R1 consistently improves reasoning accuracy and generation quality over existing GraphRAG methods.

Large Language Models (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable performance across a broad range of natural language processing

Retrieval-Augmented Genera-tion (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) mitigates this issue by augmenting additional information from exter-nal knowledge sources to the inputs of the model. However, most existing RAG frameworks retrieve text chunks (Lewis et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025), overlooking the relational struc-ture among entities and documents. To address this limitation, recent GraphRAG approaches (Edge et al., 2024; Han et al., 2025) organize knowledge into entity–relation graphs, enabling more struc-tured retrieval and multi-step reasoning. To enhance GraphRAG frameworks, a rein-forcement learning (RL)-based agentic GraphRAG frameworks, such as Graph-R1 (Luo et al., 2025a), have been proposed inspired by the success of DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025a) and Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025). These approaches adopt a multi-step retrieval strategy that empowers LLMs to iteratively perform reasoning steps with external knowledge. The agent LLM is optimized via re-inforcement learning using sparse, outcome-level rewards. Despite their promise, existing RL-based agentic GraphRAG frameworks face two key chal-lenges: (i) Overreliance on contextual similarity in graph retrieval, overlooking relational structures and topological proximities inherent in knowledge graphs; and (ii) Sparse and delayed reward sig-nals that fail to adequately capture the quality of intermediate reasoning steps, thereby hindering ef-fective learning in complex, multi-step retrieval scenarios. To deal with these challenges, we propose ProGraph-R1, a step-level progress-aware rein-forcement learning framework that enhances multi-turn graph retrieval-augmented generation in multi-hop knowledge-intensive tasks. ProGraph-R1 in-troduces a graph structure-aware retrieval module

that jointly considers semantic similarity and struc-tural proximity within a knowledge graph, enabling more accurate and contextually coherent subgraph retrieval. Furthermore, we design a progress-based step-wise policy optimization that assigns dense signals to intermediate reasoning steps. Specifi-cally, intermediate retrieval actions are rewarded based on their contribution to reasoning progress, which is measured by structural consistency with the reasoning path and uncertainty reduction to-ward the target answer, rather than relying solely on final outcome rewards. (2) We design a step progress-aware reward reshaping that provides fine-grained rewards on intermediate reasoning steps based on the informativeness and coherence of each step within a graph. (3) Our ProGraph-R1 achieves the best performance com-pared to existing GraphRAG and RL-based agen-tic frameworks on multi-hop question answering datasets. 

2 Related Works 

2.1 Graph Retrieval–Augmented Generation 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023) has been introduced to deal with non-factual or hallucinated responses generated by large language models. GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) has extended the RAG paradigm by lever-aging knowledge graphs to provide richer con-text and improve complex reasoning. Following GraphRAG, Hyper-RAG (Feng et al., 2025b) and HypergraphRAG (Luo et al., 2025b) harness the power of hypergraphs, enabling the capture of higher-order relationships beyond simple pairwise links. In parallel, another line of work (Chen et al., 2025; Guo et al., 2025b; Gutiérrez et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2025) explores re-trieval mechanisms that emphasize computational efficiency without sacrificing reasoning accuracy. More recently, Graph-R1 (Luo et al., 2025a) further extends graph RAG by leveraging reinforcement learning to enhance adaptability and reasoning per-formance. 

2.2 Reinforcement Fine-Tuning for LLM 

Reinforcement learning (RL), such as RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022), has emerged as a key technique to boost LLM reasoning capabilities, either by optimizing for outcome-based rewards or by using preference feedback. For example, DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025a) is trained with the Group Relative Policy Op-timization (GRPO), which evaluates multiple outputs from the model to compute relative advantages, thereby eliminating the need for a separate value model for Generalized Advantage Estimation (Schulman et al., 2015). In addition, several works have explored agentic reinforcement learning frameworks (Zheng et al., 2025; Feng et al., 2025a; Zeng et al., 2025), enabling LLMs to automatically decide when to invoke tools, thereby significantly improving their multi-turn interaction and tool-use capabilities. Motivated by the success of reinforcement fine-tuning approaches, Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) leverages multi-turn interleaved reasoning and search by dynamically retrieving external information, while R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025) employs a two-stage, outcome-based reinforcement learning scheme that enables LLMs to invoke search engines during reasoning. 

3 ProGraph-R1

We begin by describing the background of a RL-based GraphRAG frame-work, including the group-relative policy optimiza-tion formulation (Guo et al., 2025a), which serves as the foundation of our training strategy. Next, we present our inter-turn entity connectiveness-based step-wise policy optimization that enables the agent LLM to iteratively perform multi-step reasoning and retrieval, progressively refining its knowledge-grounded generation. 3.3 Step Progress-Aware Policy Optimization 

Most prior RL-optimized RAG frameworks (Jin et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025a) treat retrieval as an intermediate step and optimize the policy primarily with sparse and sequence -level supervision. All step (turn)-level decisions within a reasoning trace share the same learning signal regardless of the quality of each step. This uniform signal assign-ment may over-reinforce correct trajectories with wrong reasoning or noisy retrieval actions. Moreover, existing methods typically do not con-sider the quality of retrieved evidence in the opti-mization process. In knowledge-intensive tasks, a final answer accuracy depends not only on the model’s generation policy but also on whether the retrieved information is relevant and consis-tent with the evolving reasoning state. However, most existing works neglect this interaction, which makes the policy rely on spurious correlations in retrieved contexts or fail to correct the retrieval behavior. To address these limitations, we propose a retrieval-aware step-level policy optimization framework where the agent iteratively generates sub-queries qt and interacts with a knowledge hy-pergraph GK . Our ProGraph-R1 can be formulated as a step-wise reasoning process where the agent iteratively generates sub-queries qt for each turn t

and interacts with a knowledge hypergraph. 

Step progress-based dense rewarding. We ap-ply the dense reward scoring mechanism to reflect the step-level progress. We design the progress score rSP t at step t, which captures the certainty of reaching ground-truth outputs y∗ after generating the current intermediate step: 

rsp t = P (y∗ | s≤t, G≤t) − P (y∗ | s<t , G<t ). (11) We estimate P (y∗ | st, Gt) by sampling multiple output sequences conditioned on the current history and retrieved context and averaging the resulting outcome reward values. 

Structure-consistent progressive dense reward-ing. While rSP t quantifies the informativeness of the intermediate thoughts at current step t, it does not enforce graph-consistent progress in multi-hop reasoning. We therefore introduce retrieval-aware reward shaping terms that encourage progress along coherent chains in the knowledge hyper-graph. 5 Conclusion 

We have introduced ProGraph-R1, a step-aware re-inforcement learning framework for graph-based retrieval and reasoning. While prior GraphRAG frameworks advanced agentic multi-step reasoning, they remain limited by structural unawareness and sparse reward feedback. ProGraph-R1 overcomes these issues through structure-aware retrieval and step-wise advantage modulation, enabling more coherent reasoning. Experiments on knowledge-intensive QA benchmarks show that ProGraph-R1 consistently improves factual grounding over exist-ing GraphRAG and RL-based baselines. 

Limitations 

ProGraph-R1 method is significantly influenced by the prompt, retriever, the base LLM, and the given input graph. Specifically, based on the structure and quality of the input graph, the downstream task performance can vary substantially. 

Ethics Statement 

As we use LLM with external knowledge bases, the outputs may contain unintentionally hallucinating, harmful, sensitive, or biased content. Therefore, the LLM and the knowledge base should be care-fully selected to evade such situations.
