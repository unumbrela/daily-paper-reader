Abstract 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a dominant paradigm for mitigat-ing hallucinations in Large Language Models (LLMs) by incorporating external knowledge. Nevertheless, effectively integrating and inter-preting key evidence scattered across noisy documents remains a critical challenge for ex-isting RAG systems. In this paper, we pro-pose GraphAnchor, a novel Graph-Anchored Knowledge Indexing approach that reconceptu-alizes graph structures from static knowledge representations into active, evolving knowl-edge indices. GraphAnchor incrementally up-dates a graph during iterative retrieval to anchor salient entities and relations, yielding a struc-tured index that guides the LLM in evaluating knowledge sufficiency and formulating subse-quent subqueries. The final answer is generated by jointly leveraging all retrieved documents and the final evolved graph. Experiments on four multi-hop question answering benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of GraphAnchor, and reveal that GraphAnchor modulates the LLM’s attention to more effectively associate key information distributed in retrieved doc-uments. All code and data are available at 

https://github.com/NEUIR/GraphAnchor .

1 Introduction 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) has emerged as a promising paradigm for mitigating hallucinations in Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020). For a given query, typical RAG models retrieve relevant doc-uments, which are incorporated as contextual ev-idence to guide LLMs in generating accurate an-swers (Ram et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). Although RAG models have demonstrated effectiveness across many knowledge-intensive

The dis-persed nature of key information across retrieved documents poses a challenge for RAG models in effectively integrating and leveraging the retrieved knowledge (Kuratov et al., 2024). To address this issue, numerous studies have ex-plored structuring retrieved knowledge to better aggregate supporting evidence distributed across multiple passages (Li et al., 2024c, 2025b). These approaches typically investigate the effectiveness of different representation formats for extracting and representing salient information from retrieved evidence (Wu et al., 2025a; Li et al., 2024c; Yu et al., 2025). Although these methods enhance the coherence of knowledge representations and help reduce noise in the retrieved contexts, they primar-ily leverage these structures to filter out irrelevant information and assist LLMs in better utilizing the retrieved knowledge. fully exploit crucial evidence that cannot be cap-tured in these formats and overlook the role of struc-tured data in facilitating deeper interpretation and reasoning over the retrieved knowledge (Pankin, 2013). As shown in Figure 1, graphs provide a well-defined structural representation for explicitly modeling entities and their relations. Such graphs can be incrementally updated by introducing newly identified entities, for example, adding “Carbon County” and linking it with “Red Lodge” through the “county seat” relation. This graph updating process progressively interprets the retrieved doc-uments, which motivates us to treat the graph as a knowledge index for RAG systems. This paper presents GraphAnchor, a Graph-Anchored Knowledge Indexing approach that it-eratively updates a graph to enhance the interpreta-tion of retrieved knowledge during the iterative re-trieval process. Unlike previous methods (Wu et al., 2025a; Li et al., 2024c; Yu et al., 2025), GraphAn-chor redefines the role of the graph, shifting it from a mere knowledge representation to an active in-dexing tool. At each retrieval step, GraphAnchor first retrieves documents based on the current query and updates the graph based on these retrieved doc-uments. Then GraphAnchor assesses whether the current knowledge, informed by the graph, is suf-ficient to answer the query. If the knowledge is lacking, a new query is generated. Once the avail-able knowledge is sufficient, the final response is generated according to the documents retrieved at each step with the final version of the graph. Our experiments on four multi-hop QA datasets with different backbone LLMs demonstrate the ef-fectiveness of GraphAnchor. Rather than refining knowledge for question answering, GraphAnchor focuses on knowledge anchoring and improves both the retrieval and QA modules within an itera-tive RAG framework. Specifically, GraphAnchor facilitates the retrieval of more relevant and diverse documents across multiple retrieval steps, and en-ables LLMs to better interpret and utilize the re-trieved knowledge. Further analysis shows that, as the retrieval process progresses, the graph anchors an increasing number of related entities and rela-tions into the index, and subsequently modulates the attention mechanism of LLMs to aggregate dis-tributed evidence from the retrieved documents. 

2 Related Work 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) models typically retrieve relevant passages and incorpo-rate them as contextual inputs, thereby helping Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate ac-curate and grounded responses (Ram et al., 2023; Lewis et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023; Shuster et al., 2021). However, conventional RAG approaches often suffer from insufficient knowledge acquisi-tion, particularly in multi-hop question answering scenarios where reasoning requires the integration of evidence across multiple passages. To mitigate this limitation, several studies have proposed iterative retrieval strategies that progres-sively seek and accumulate relevant knowledge to support answer generation (Trivedi et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). In addition, some methods perform deep retrieval by interacting with external retrieval tools during the reasoning process, enabling more flexible and adaptive infor-mation seeking (Jin et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a). Nevertheless, as the number of retrieval steps in-creases, these RAG systems inevitably introduce more noisy or irrelevant information, which can substantially degrade the quality of the generated outputs (Cuconasu et al., 2024). To improve knowledge utilization in LLMs, prior work has explored reranking retrieved pas-sages (Asai et al., 2024) or summarizing salient information to filter out noise (Vig et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2025b). However, these approaches typi-cally process retrieved passages in isolation, with limited modeling of relationships among different documents. As a result, cross-document evidence may be overlooked or insufficiently captured, par-ticularly in settings that require aggregating com-plementary information across multiple sources (Li et al., 2024c). To better aggregate diverse knowl-edge from multiple retrieved passages, a grow-ing body of work leverages structured represen-tations, including schemas, tables, and graphs, to explicitly extract key information scattered across these retrieved documents (Edge et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025b, 2024c; Fang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025). Liu et al. (2025) further explores multiple reasoning paths over the constructed knowledge graph, identifying conflicting paths as input con-text, thereby alleviating knowledge conflicts be-tween internal and external information. 3 Methodology 

As illustrated in Figure 2, this section presents the proposed GraphAnchor, which introduces an evolving graph to anchor knowledge within an iterative RAG framework. We first describe the overall architecture of GraphAnchor, highlighting how the evolving graph is integrated to support reasoning, query generation, and question answer-ing (Sec. 3.1). We then elaborate on the construc-tion and dynamic updating of the graph, which functions as a context-aware knowledge index to enhance the knowledge association and compre-hension capabilities of LLMs (Sec. 3.2). 

3.1 Graph-based Knowledge Anchoring for Iterative RAG 

Given an initial query q0 that requires multi-hop retrieval, existing approaches (Shao et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023) typically perform iterative re-trieval to progressively accumulate evidence for answering the query. Different from these meth-ods, GraphAnchor introduces an evolving knowl-edge graph G as a structured indexing mechanism, which simultaneously supports reasoning, next-query generation and final answer prediction. At the t-th retrieval step, GraphAnchor retrieves a set of relevant documents Dt conditioned on the previous query qt−1:

Dt = Retriever (qt−1), (1) where 1 ≤ t ≤ T and T denotes the maximum number of retrieval iterations. The evolving graph is then updated by incorporating the newly re-trieved documents Dt:

Gt = M(q0, D t, o t−1), (2) where M denotes the LLM employed for RAG modeling. The previous output ot−1 consists of the graph Gt−1, the intermediate reasoning result 

Rt−1, and the query qt−1. Conditioned on the up-dated graph Gt, the retrieved documents Dt, and the previous output ot−1, the LLM produces an in-termediate reasoning result Rt along with the next query qt:

Rt, q t = M(q0, D t, o t−1, G t). (3) The reasoning result Rt contains a reasoning trace enclosed by <think> and </think> , as well as a knowledge sufficiency judgement enclosed by 

<judgement> and </judgement> . The judgement is classified as either sufficient or insufficient , which determines whether the iterative retrieval process terminates. If the judgement is insufficient , the model generates the next query qt conditioned on the current reasoning result Rt.After completing the iterative retrieval process, all retrieved documents are aggregated to form the complete document set: 

D = D1 ∪ D2 ∪ · · · ∪ Dk, (4) where k denotes the actual number of retrieval it-erations. Finally, the LLM generates the answer 

Y to the original query q0 based on the aggregated documents D and the final evolving graph Gk:

Y = M(Instruct Gen , q 0, D, G k). (5) As the number of retrieval iterations increases, the aggregated document set D inevitably accumulates noisy and redundant information. In contrast, the evolving graph G is progressively refined at each iteration (see Sec. 3.2 for details), acting as a struc-tured knowledge anchor that preserves salient facts and relational structures. By grounding reasoning on this graph-based representation, the model can more effectively organize and interpret retrieved knowledge, leading to improved reasoning accu-racy and more reliable answer generation by LLMs. 

3.2 Stepwise Graph Evolution for Context-Aware Knowledge Indexing 

In this subsection, we first introduce the definition and representation of the evolving graph G, and then present our stepwise graph update mechanism. 

Graph Representation. During graph construc-tion, the LLM is tasked with extracting entities and the relations among them. Accordingly, the graph is defined as G = ( V, E), where V = {v1, . . . , v n}

denotes the set of entities, and E = {t1, . . . , t m}

denotes the set of relations extracted from docu-ments, represented as RDF triples (Klyne, 2004). Following prior work (Li et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2024), we linearize the graph G by verbalizing both its entities and triples: Entities: X(v1), X (v2), . . . , X (vn); 

Relations: X(t1), X (t2), . . . , X (tm), (6) where X(vi) denotes an entity verbalization func-tion that encodes the entity name together with its associated attributes, and X(tj ) denotes the verbal-ized RDF triple tj , consisting of a head entity, a relation, and a tail entity. The resulting linearized graph representation is wrapped with the special tokens <graph> and </graph> .

Stepwise Graph Evolution. During iterative re-trieval, GraphAnchor adopts a stepwise graph evo-lution mechanism to maintain the graph G, which functions as a dynamic knowledge index for both model reasoning and subsequent query generation. As formalized in Eq. 2, the graph at step t is updated based on the previous graph Gt−1, the rea-soning trace Rt−1, and the generated query qt−1:

Gt = M(q0, D t, {Gt−1, R t−1, q t−1}). (7) Specifically, when transitioning from Gt−1 to Gt,the graph is incrementally updated by incorporating the retrieved documents Dt at step t, resulting in a stepwise evolutionary process: 

∆Gt−1→t = Index (Dt, {Rt−1, q t−1}), (8) where Index (·) denotes a document indexing op-eration conditioned on the prior reasoning trace 

Rt−1 and the previously issued query qt−1. These two signals jointly guide the extraction of salient, query-relevant information from Dt for graph up-dating. As a result, the graph progressively inte-grates query-aware evidence across retrieval steps, allowing key entities and relations to be incremen-tally identified, highlighted, and anchored within the evolving graph structure.

5.4 Attention Visualization by Incorporating Knowledge Anchoring 

In this subsection, we explore how the con-structed graph assists LLMs in anchoring knowl-edge through the attention mechanism. We analyze the attention distribution when incorporating the constructed graphs for knowledge indexing. In-stances from HotpotQA that require the maximum number of retrieval steps (4 steps) are selected to illustrate the knowledge anchoring mechanism. 

Attention Distribution with Graph Anchoring. 

As shown in Figure 5, we aggregate all retrieved documents as context and visualize the attention distribution on document tokens with graphs con-structed at different retrieval steps. First, we show the attention proportions of both retrieved documents and the graph during answer generation in Figure 5(a) to examine the role of the constructed graph. The results indicate that the graph typically receives the majority of atten-tion from the LLM during question answering, and that attention weights increase as the graph is updated through more retrieval iterations. This demonstrates that the graph plays a crucial role in helping LLMs better answer queries, confirming its effectiveness in interpreting retrieved knowledge. 6 Conclusion 

This paper introduces GraphAnchor, a novel graph-based knowledge anchoring approach that reformu-lates the graph structure from a refined knowledge representation into an evolutive index for RAG. By dynamically updating the graph at each retrieval step, the proposed graph enables LLMs to anchor a broader set of related entities for knowledge utiliza-tion, which offers a new perspective for alleviating knowledge conflicts in LLMs. 

Limitations 

Although GraphAnchor demonstrates effectiveness in knowledge indexing for RAG systems, the qual-ity of the constructed graph index remains con-strained by the capabilities of the underlying LLM. Specifically, the performance of the proposed graph construction and utilization depends on the model’s ability to accurately extract, structure, and organize relevant information from retrieved documents. In addition, the current implementation represents the graph solely through verbalized entities and rela-tions; more expressive and structured graph rep-resentation methods may further enhance perfor-mance.
